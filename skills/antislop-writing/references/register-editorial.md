# Editorial register

Apply alongside the core directives for op-eds, blog posts, columns, and persuasive essays.
The core still governs — lead with the point, cut filler, name sources, no significance inflation.
What changes is the tell threshold: this genre trades in voice and rhetoric, so devices that read as tics in a memo can be legitimate craft here in moderation.

## The target

A signed argument with a person behind it: a well-edited op-ed, a columnist with a recognizable voice, an essay that earns its position.
It has a point of view and defends it.
It is not a hot take, a thread, or a thought-leadership post that mistakes cadence for substance.

## Deltas from the core

A point of view is the job here, not a lapse.
Where the professional baseline stays neutral, an op-ed takes a position and argues it: state the thesis early and let everything serve it.
Structure the piece around that argument — open with a lede that earns attention honestly and a clear claim, build the case, and close on the strongest point or a real implication, not a sweeping prophecy or a signposted summary.

The tell thresholds recalibrate, but they do not disappear.
A single antithesis, one metaphor, or one well-placed short sentence can land a point; the failure is the loop — three antitheses in a stretch, the same metaphor ten times, a page of fragments.
Use a device, make it earn its place, and move on.
Voice comes from specifics and stance, not from cadence: a real opinion, concrete evidence, and a claim you are willing to be wrong about, never a voice manufactured with punchy fragments, rhetorical questions, and "here's the kicker" — the same moves the rhetorical-stance tells in `ai-tells.md` flag when they are faked.

Persuasion tempts stakes inflation, so argue the actual consequence at its real scale; a concrete, defensible claim persuades better than "this will reshape everything."

## What still fails here

The recalibration is narrow.
Significance and stakes inflation, promotional adjectives, false vulnerability, and "the truth is simple" remain tells at any threshold.
An op-ed needs sources more than a memo does, not fewer, so vague attribution still fails, as do sweeping closers and generic optimism.
And one-point dilution still fails: a strong 800-word argument padded to 3,000 is weaker, not more comprehensive.
